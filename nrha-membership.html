The National Reining Horse Association incorporated on November 30, 1966, in Coshocton, Ohio. Its first Futurity that same year drew roughly 35 competitors to Columbus, Ohio. Six decades later, NRHA sanctions more than 1,400 approved shows annually across the United States, Canada, and more than 35 additional countries, with nearly $30 million paid out to competitors in 2024 alone.

This page tracks the long arc of membership growth — from a regional Midwestern association in its first two decades, to an international governing body — and breaks down the member mix by type: General, Non Pro, Professionals, Youth, and Associate. All figures are drawn from NRHA's own published milestones and its 2024 Statistics Overview.

The Long Arc: 1966–2024

60 Years
Membership Milestones
Key anchor points from NRHA's historic record
1966
Founded November 30 in Coshocton, Ohio. First Futurity draws ~35 competitors in Columbus, OH. Sire & Dam Program launched same year.
1966–1985
The Regional Era. NRHA remains a mostly Midwestern and East Coast association. Kaye Potts, the association's first office manager, recalled: "Every year saw an increase in membership and in Futurity entries. There never was a big surge, just more interest and participation." Membership grows from dozens of founding members to several thousand by the mid-1980s.
1986
Futurity moves to Oklahoma City. The relocation opens the sport to riders from the Southwest and Pacific Coast — a turning point in national reach. Hall of Fame established. Youth classes added to NRHA competition.
1987
International German Championship becomes the first NRHA-approved event in Europe.
1989
First NRHA Competition License issued — the separate horse registration that still underpins every sanctioned show today.
1998
NRHA office relocates to Oklahoma City. Reining accepted as the first western discipline of the United States Equestrian Team (USET).
2000
Membership tops 10,000 for the first time. Federation Equestre International (FEI) accepts reining for international competition the same year.
2001
National Reining Horse Youth Association (NRHyA) formed.
2008
50,000th Competition License issued. NRHA Futurity entries reach an all-time high of 945 horses.
2009
NRHA welcomes its 60,000th cumulative member. First European Futurity held in Kreuth, Germany.
2011
70,000th cumulative membership completed — 11 years after crossing 10,000 active.
2020
COVID dip. Active membership falls to 12,729 as shows cancel worldwide. Show counts drop more than 50 percent at the peak of restrictions.
2021
V-shaped recovery. Membership rebounds 10 percent to 14,000 — the Taylor Sheridan / Yellowstone / Run For A Million effect fuels unprecedented interest. Entries climb to an all-time high of 112,929.
2023
All-time peak: 14,646 active members. Highest total in association history.
2024
Slight consolidation to 14,396 — 1.7 percent below peak, still the second-highest on record. Nearly $30 million in prize money paid to competitors.
2026
60th Anniversary. NRHA and the NRHA Futurity both celebrate six decades — a rare dual milestone in the western performance horse industry.

Breakdown by Member Type

General / Youth / Associate

NRHA offers five distinct membership types. General covers adult riders and owners and is by far the largest category. Youth membership is available to competitors 18 and younger. Associate membership is a lower-priced entry point restricted to Category 10 (Green Reiner and Ride & Slide) classes. Professionals and Non Pro are competitive designations layered on top of General membership — a Non Pro Declaration converts a General member into a Non Pro competitor, while a Professionals application converts them into a competing Professional.

Non Pro vs. Professional: The Competing Classes

Trainers & Amateurs

These are the two competitive designations that matter most at the show pen. A Non Pro cannot train or show horses for remuneration in any discipline, and must file a Non Pro Declaration. A Professional trains and competes for a living and must sign the Professional Code of Ethics. Non Pro members outnumber Professionals by roughly five to one — a ratio that has held remarkably steady for the past five years.

Non Pro: The Amateur Core

Non Pro membership bottomed at 8,257 in 2020, then climbed three consecutive years to 9,046 in 2023 — a 9.6 percent gain. The 2024 softening to 8,823 mirrors the small dip in overall membership. Non Pro is where most of the reining world actually competes: amateurs who own their horses, hire trainers, and haul to shows on their own dime.

Professionals: Stable Trainer Class

The Professional count has hovered between 1,786 and 1,833 for four straight years after its 2020 low of 1,643. This stability is a healthy signal — the sport supports a mature, established trainer class large enough to serve nearly 15,000 members across 1,400+ shows, but not so large that the market is saturated.

Why youth numbers matter

The National Reining Horse Youth Association (NRHyA) was formed in 2001 as the sport's formal pipeline. Youth membership grew every year from 2020 through 2023, reaching 1,464 members — the highest in recent history. The 2024 pullback to 1,415 represents a 3.3 percent dip but still sits above every year before 2023. For a discipline-specific equine youth organization, a stable 1,200–1,500 base is a strong indicator of long-term health: these are the members who will become tomorrow's Non Pros and Professionals.

A genuinely international association

One in three NRHA members now lives outside the United States. International membership has grown 17.6 percent since 2020 (3,943 to 4,635), with Canada growing even faster at 41.6 percent over the same period (750 to 1,062). The NRHA European Futurity in Cremona, Italy, now ranks among the richest reinings in the world — the 2024 edition added $1 million in prize money, the first European event ever to do so. Italy, Germany, Brazil, France, Austria, Belgium, the Netherlands, Switzerland, Sweden, Poland, Mexico, Australia, Israel, and South Africa are all home to active NRHA-affiliated associations.

Membership is only part of the story

Member counts can flatten while the sport around them accelerates. Between 2020 and 2024, NRHA membership rose 13.1 percent — but total annual entries climbed 89.0 percent, added money more than tripled (+229 percent, from $6.5M to $21.4M), and approved shows more than doubled (722 to 1,493). The typical NRHA member in 2024 is showing more often, in richer classes, and across a wider geographic footprint than their 2020 counterpart. That engagement-per-member trend may matter more than the raw headcount.

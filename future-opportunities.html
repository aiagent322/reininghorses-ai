Structural Proposal · 2026–2030

Future Opportunities

A handicap system that borrows from polo. A team format that borrows from team roping. A proposal to rebuild reining's competitive structure for the next twenty years — by creating camaraderie, new entry points, and new reasons to compete.

The two companion pages on this site — NRHA Membership and the Fool N School Case Study — document the problem. True amateur membership has been flat since 2021. The customer-per-trainer ratio has declined. Entry-level participation peaked in 2022 and has fallen for two consecutive years. The sport needs a structural answer, not another marketing campaign.

This page lays out one. The proposal has two parts, both borrowed from proven models in adjacent equestrian sports: a handicap-number system in the style of polo and team roping, overlaid on NRHA's existing class structure rather than replacing it; and a Team Reining format where two riders compete as a pair with accumulative scoring, opening up new class configurations that don't exist in reining today. Neither piece requires eliminating anything NRHA currently does. Both pieces are additive, low-risk, and high-upside.

Why Handicaps Work: Polo & Team Roping

The Model

Two of the oldest and most durable handicap systems in equestrian sport belong to polo (codified in 1890 by the United States Polo Association) and team roping (codified through the USTRC's TRIAD system in the 1980s). Both systems solve the same problem: how do you keep a sport competitive, welcoming, and growing when players range from brand-new beginners to world-class professionals? Both answers are structurally identical.

What the handicap system actually does

A handicap accomplishes three things at once: it tells every rider where they stand, it tells every show producer which class to put them in, and it tells every new entrant that they will compete against other new entrants. NRHA's current system — four Open levels, four Non Pro levels, Rookie 1/2, Green Reiner 1/2, Short Stirrup, Prime Time — theoretically accomplishes the first two, but the structure is fragmented, the eligibility rules are multi-dimensional, and most riders couldn't state their "level" without consulting a trainer. A single published handicap number solves that in one stroke and makes the team format below possible.

Important: nothing existing is removed

The current NRHA level and class structure remains in place. A Level 4 Non Pro is still a Level 4 Non Pro. The handicap tier (6 in this case) is an additional number that gets printed on the membership card — published, portable, and useful in two specific ways: as a qualification marker for new team-format classes, and as a single-glance credibility signal at any NRHA-approved show.

Team Reining: The Partner Format

New Class Family

Team roping transformed itself from a rodeo event into a 10,000-event industry by doing one thing: making the sport a partnership. You need a header AND a heeler. You pick your partner. You enter your handicap pair. You succeed together or fail together. The interpersonal dynamics — choosing a partner, splitting winnings, making friends at the arena, developing a working relationship — are the secret ingredient that keeps people coming back. Reining has never had this.

The proposal: Team Reining. Two riders enter as a unit. Each rider shows a pattern individually, judged under existing NRHA rules. Scores are added together. The combined score determines placings and payouts. Team handicap (the sum of both riders' individual handicaps) determines which bracket class the team qualifies for.

Team Reining Bracket Classes

5 New Classes

Five bracket classes, each with a combined-handicap cap. A team's total handicap determines which bracket they're eligible for, and most teams can enter only the highest bracket they qualify for (or optionally move up). This is borrowed directly from team roping's #8, #10, #12 structure and polo's low-goal / medium-goal / high-goal tournament tiers.

Why This Builds the Sport

Structural Benefits

What the Team Format Creates

  • Camaraderie. You show up with a partner. You practice together, travel together, support each other at the arena. The team format is the single best social glue in team roping and polo, and it's completely absent from reining.
  • Trainer-client bonding. A Pro who competes as a team with his or her Non Pro client creates shared stakes — the trainer doesn't just coach, they compete alongside. Strengthens the core customer-trainer relationship that sustains every training barn.
  • Family participation. Husband-wife, parent-child, sibling pairs. Team reining gives families a reason to all show up and all compete together as a single entry.
  • New revenue with zero new events. Every existing NRHA show can add team classes to its class list. No new sanctioning, no new venues, no new judges required. Estimated $1.2M+ in new annual entry revenue from 1 team class per show at 1,493 shows.
  • Social-media storytelling. "The Flarida-Dawson Team" is a better headline than "Shawn Flarida wins another Level 4 Open." Pairings create narrative.
  • Clearer beginner entry. A Green Reiner can enter the #4 Beginner Bracket with another beginner. No intimidation. No unfavorable matchups. No guesswork about eligibility.
  • Published handicap number. One number on your membership card that tells every producer, every partner, and every bracket exactly where you belong.

Honest Challenges to Address

  • Handicap committee workload. USPA's handicap committee reviews ~5,000 players annually. NRHA's committee would need to review ~1,800 Pros and ~9,000 Non Pros at first. Can be phased: start with earnings-based auto-assignment, add human review at tier 6 and above.
  • Sandbagging risk. Any handicap system invites people to game their rating. Solution: USTRC's "observation-based reclassification" clause, plus annual public review.
  • Show producer logistics. Adding team classes to an already long show day. Solution: team classes run Saturday afternoon only; patterns are shared with individual classes already on the schedule.
  • Initial education effort. The first 12–18 months of rollout require sustained communication. Handicap cards must be mailed to all current members in 2026. Every 2026 show must include a printed bracket-class explainer.
  • Payout structure. New decisions required on 50/50 team splits vs. added-money purses. Solution: follow team roping's default (50/50 split with optional added-money incentive sidepots).
  • Buy-in from top Pros. Some elite Pros will resist anything that gives up scheduling control. Solution: let the 2027 pilot program be opt-in for shows, then let popular demand drive the 2028 expansion.

The math is straightforward and small

Every NRHA-approved show in 2024 (1,493 worldwide) adding a single Team Reining class with an average of 8 teams entered at a $100 combined entry fee generates $1.19 million in gross new entry revenue — before any added-money incentives, before sponsor dollars, and before growth from incremental show days. Adding three bracket classes per show at mid-estimate participation gets to $3.6 million. For reference, that would be roughly a 12 percent lift on 2024's $29 million total money-won figure — achieved through format innovation, not more shows, more sponsors, or richer futurities.

Crucially, this revenue is incremental. It doesn't cannibalize existing class entries; the same rider who shows Non Pro on Saturday can enter a team class on Saturday afternoon. It's a second opportunity to compete at the same show, for roughly the same overhead, against a different field.

Implementation Roadmap

Three Phases
A Three-Year Rollout
Designed to be additive, low-risk, and testable before full commitment
Phase 1 — 2027: Handicap Assignment & Pilot
Q1: NRHA Eligibility Committee adopts the 12-tier ladder. Every current member receives a handicap letter based on 2024-2026 earnings auto-assigned. Q2: Handicap cards mailed to all members. ReinerSuite updated to display handicap. Q3-Q4: Pilot program at ~20 volunteer affiliate shows across the United States running one or two Team Reining bracket classes (#8 and #12) with a $100 combined entry, 50/50 payout split. Success metrics: entries, participant survey, Pro/NP pairing demographics.
Phase 2 — 2028: Scale & Standardize
Pilot review: Public results published Q1 2028. Adjustments: Refine handicap tiers, formalize bracket caps, publish official Team Reining Handbook supplement. Expansion: Team Reining classes become optional but sanctioned at all NRHA-approved shows. Affiliate championships adopt at least one bracket class. Major events: NRHA Derby introduces an invitational #18 Open Bracket with added money as a 2028 pilot.
Phase 3 — 2029–2030: Full Integration
NRHA Futurity: Hosts first full Team Reining Championship with all five bracket classes, added money above $500,000 total. Handicap governance: Annual review cycle becomes standard, publishing movement up and down the ladder in Reiner magazine. Affiliates: Team Reining becomes a standard class family at every affiliate championship. International: European Derby and European Futurity adopt the same structure with FEI-compatible bracket caps.

Why Now — and Why This

Closing Argument

The structural answer, not another marketing campaign

Reining does not have a marketing problem. Yellowstone, The Last Cowboy, and The Run For A Million gave the sport more free marketing than any other western discipline has ever received. Reining has a structural problem: the competitive format, the on-ramps, and the social mechanics haven't been redesigned in forty years. Team roping redesigned its structure in the 1980s and became a 10,000-event industry. Polo codified its structure in 1890 and survived two world wars, three cultural upheavals, and the rise of every other equestrian sport to still be thriving 135 years later.

The proposal on this page is additive, low-cost, low-risk, and borrowed from models that are already proven. It doesn't require NRHA to change anything it's already doing. It requires NRHA to add one number to every membership card, write a chapter of new rules, and let its 1,493 annual shows experiment with a new way to compete. If the experiment works, the 2030 reining industry looks like the team roping industry of today — broader, deeper, more socially sticky, and more financially stable. If it doesn't, NRHA has lost nothing that wasn't replaceable.

The cost of doing nothing is knowable: the data on the companion pages tells that story. Flat amateur growth. Declining customers per trainer. A household base of roughly 6,250 worldwide. A sport this small cannot afford to not try.

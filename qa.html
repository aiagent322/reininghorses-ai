01

THE SPORT OF REINING

18 Questions
What is reining?+

Reining is a western riding competition in which horse and rider execute a precise, pre-selected pattern of maneuvers — large fast circles, small slow circles, flying lead changes, rollbacks, 360-degree spins in place, and the signature sliding stop. All work is performed at the lope or gallop. Horses must be willingly guided with little or no apparent resistance.

Often called "the dressage of the western world," reining showcases the athleticism and trainability of the working ranch horse. The sport originated from the practical skills cowboys needed to manage cattle on open rangeland.

Where did reining come from?+

Reining evolved from the practical demands of ranch work in the American West. Cowboys needed horses that could stop instantly to cut a calf from the herd, spin on a dime to change direction, and run at full speed when chasing cattle. These functional skills became formalized into competitive events as ranching culture grew, with cowhands challenging each other to see whose horse could stop harder and spin faster.

The National Reining Horse Association was founded in 1966 to standardize rules and sanctioned competition. Since then reining has spread worldwide and became an FEI-recognized equestrian discipline (though FEI and NRHA parted ways in 2021 over governance differences).

How is reining different from dressage?+

Both sports judge horse and rider on precise, memorized patterns of movement, but the execution and philosophy differ fundamentally. Reining horses work on a completely loose rein — the horse must respond to leg and seat cues alone, with no contact on the bit. Dressage uses consistent rein contact throughout. Reining maneuvers are explosive and high-speed; dressage focuses on collected, elevated movement. Reining rewards power, speed control, and sliding stops; dressage rewards collection, cadence, and impulsion.

Is reining a recognized Olympic sport?+

Not currently. Reining was included in the 2002 World Equestrian Games under FEI governance, but the FEI removed reining from its program in 2021 due to conflicts between the FEI's international governance model and the NRHA's structure. NRHA continues to govern the sport independently and remains the world's largest governing body for reining with 1,140+ approved shows annually. The sport is recognized as a national discipline by the United States Equestrian Federation (USEF).

What is the National Reining Horse Association (NRHA)?+

The NRHA is the international governing body for the sport of reining, founded in 1966 and headquartered in Oklahoma City, Oklahoma. The organization sanctions over 1,140 approved shows per year, sets judging standards, issues rulebooks, and runs the sport's two premier events — the NRHA Futurity (December) and the 6666 NRHA Derby (June). In 2026, NRHA celebrates its 60th anniversary alongside the 60th NRHA Futurity. The organization pays out nearly $24 million in prizes annually. Visit nrha.com to join, find shows, or access the official handbook.

How big is the reining industry?+

Reining is one of the fastest-growing western performance disciplines. The NRHA sanctions 1,140+ shows per year across the USA and internationally, with over 250 Entry Level events. The sport pays out nearly $24 million in prizes annually. The 2025 NRHA Futurity set an all-time purse record at $3.9 million combined. The Run For A Million, co-owned by Yellowstone creator Taylor Sheridan, offers a $1 million purse and is credited with bringing reining to mainstream audiences through the reality series "The Last Cowboy."

What is the sliding stop?+

The sliding stop is reining's signature maneuver and its most visually dramatic moment. The horse accelerates to a full gallop down the arena, then on cue from the rider plants both hind feet firmly in the ground and skids to a controlled halt — sometimes sliding 20 to 30 feet or more. While the hind legs are locked and sliding, the front legs continue to walk forward beneath the horse. The horse drops its hindquarters, breaks at the loin, lowers its head and neck, and stays mobile in the front. Reining horses wear special smooth-soled sliding plates on their hind feet to maximize the slide distance.

What is a spin (turnaround) in reining?+

A spin is a series of 360-degree turns executed over a stationary inside hind leg. The horse pivots around its planted rear pivot foot, crossing the front legs over each other with moderate to fast speed. NRHA patterns typically call for 4 spins in one direction, then 4 in the other. Spins are judged on speed, correctness of the pivot foot, degree of crossing in front, and precise completion of the required number of rotations. Over or under-spinning by one-eighth turn results in a half-point penalty.

What is a rollback in reining?+

A rollback is a 180-degree reversal of forward motion. The horse runs to a stop, immediately rolls its shoulders back over its hocks in the opposite direction, and departs at a lope — all in one continuous, fluid motion with no hesitation. The rollback should be sharp and athletic. Judges credit horses that accelerate aggressively into the stop, snap back cleanly, and depart with energy and speed. Any hesitation or loss of momentum between the stop and departure is faulted.

What are circles in reining?+

Circles are performed at the lope and demonstrate the horse's control, willingness to guide, and the rider's ability to vary speed. NRHA patterns require both large fast circles and small slow circles, showing clear speed differentiation between the two. The horse must maintain a consistent lead throughout each circle, stay balanced, and respond to the rider's cues without resistance. Credit is given for horses that show dramatic speed differences between the large and small circles.

What is a flying lead change?+

A flying lead change is when the horse switches its leading leg (the leg that strikes forward first at the lope) while in motion, without breaking to a trot or walk. In reining, lead changes happen at the center of the arena when changing circle direction. The change must be complete — both front and hind legs must switch simultaneously. Late or simple (non-flying) lead changes result in penalties. Credit is given for flat, fluid, and simultaneous changes made aggressively yet willingly.

What is a rundown in reining?+

A rundown is the approach to a stop or rollback — the horse accelerates down the length of the arena at a full gallop before being asked to stop. Judges evaluate the quality and speed of the rundown. The horse must run past designated arena markers before stopping, and stops made short of the marker result in a penalty. A horse that runs with confidence and speed while remaining in control scores higher than one that runs cautiously or hesitantly.

What is a hesitation in reining?+

A hesitation is a brief pause required at specific points in the pattern — typically after completing a spin, after a stop, or at the beginning and end of the pattern. The hesitation allows the judge to confirm the maneuver is complete before the horse proceeds. Failure to hesitate where the pattern requires it can result in a penalty.

Can any breed of horse compete in reining?+

Yes — NRHA competition is open to all breeds. However, American Quarter Horses dominate the sport due to their natural athleticism, powerful hindquarters, low center of gravity, and trainability. American Paint Horses and Appaloosas also compete regularly. Some international competitors use Lusitano and Warmblood crosses. The breed restrictions that apply at AQHA or APHA shows do not apply at open NRHA events.

What does it mean to "rein" a horse?+

According to the NRHA, "to rein a horse is not only to guide him, but also to control his every movement. The best reined horse should be willfully guided or controlled with little or no apparent resistance and dictated to completely. Any movement on his own must be considered a lack of control." This philosophy underpins all judging — the horse must appear to perform effortlessly under complete control, as if the rider's communication is invisible.

What are sliding plates?+

Sliding plates are special horseshoes worn on a reining horse's hind feet to maximize slide distance in the stop. They are wider, longer, and smoother than standard horseshoes — with no toe clips or side clips that might catch in the ground. The smooth steel surface allows the hind hooves to glide across the arena footing during a stop. Sliding plates are only worn on the hind feet; front shoes are typically standard. The arena footing must be properly prepared (ground, groomed, and dampened) to allow maximum slide.

Is reining dangerous for horses?+

Like all athletic disciplines, reining places physical demands on horses — particularly on the hind end, stifles, hocks, and suspensory ligaments. The NRHA has extensive welfare and medication protocols to protect horse health. Proper conditioning, regular veterinary care, appropriate footing, and thoughtful training management are essential. Many reining horses compete soundly for many years. The NRHA Handbook includes detailed welfare guidelines and the organization actively enforces medication rules at all sanctioned events.

What is freestyle reining?+

Freestyle reining is a competitive class in which horse and rider perform a reining pattern of their own design set to music. Riders may add theatrical elements — costumes, props, and choreography — while still executing core reining maneuvers. Freestyle is judged on both technical merit (execution of reining maneuvers) and artistic impression (music selection, choreography, and showmanship). It is one of the most crowd-pleasing classes in the sport and is often featured at major events like the Run For A Million.

SCORING & JUDGING

16 Questions
How is reining scored?+

Every horse and rider begins with a score of 70, which represents an average performance. Judges then add or subtract points in half-point increments based on the quality of each maneuver. Scores range from +1.5 (excellent) to -1.5 (extremely poor) per maneuver on this scale: +1.5 excellent, +1.0 very good, +0.5 good, 0 average, -0.5 poor, -1.0 very poor, -1.5 extremely poor. Penalties are assessed separately for rule violations and are deducted from the final score.

What does a score of 70 mean?+

A score of 70 is the baseline — it represents an average performance where all maneuvers were completed correctly but without exceptional quality or difficulty. Scores above 70 indicate a performance that showed genuine quality, speed, control, and athletic expression. Competitive winning scores at major events typically range from 72 to 77+. Scores at or below 70 indicate a performance with noticeable flaws or penalties. A perfect pattern with maximum positive scores would theoretically reach the low-to-mid 80s.

What are the penalties in reining?+

Half-point penalties (0.5): Over or under-spinning up to 1/8 turn; out of lead for 1/4 to 1/2 circle; delayed lead change.
1-point penalties: Over or under-spinning 1/8 to 1/4 turn; out of lead for more than 1/2 circle; simple lead change; breaking gait during circles.
2-point penalties: Jogging more than half a circle; out of lead for more than 3/4 circle; running past a designated stop marker.
5-point penalties: Spurring in front of the cinch; use of two hands (except in snaffle/hackamore classes); bucking; equipment failure; holding the saddle horn.

What results in a zero score (0)?+

A zero score is assessed for major pattern violations: failure to complete the pattern as written; performing maneuvers out of sequence; over or under-spinning by more than 1/4 turn; use of two hands on split reins (outside of snaffle/hackamore classes); not running past the designated marker before stopping; fall of horse or rider; equipment failure that poses a safety hazard; and other major deviations from the prescribed pattern. A zero does not mean disqualification — the rider finishes the pattern and receives a final score of zero.

What results in a "no score" or disqualification?+

A "no score" is the equivalent of disqualification and is assessed for: abuse of the horse in the arena; excessive use of spurs or whip; misconduct by the rider; use of illegal equipment; a horse leaving the arena. A no score differs from a zero — a zero is a scored performance with major errors, while a no score is a disqualification where no score is recorded.

What does a judge look for in a sliding stop?+

Judges evaluate the stop for: horse running with speed and confidence into the stop; correct body position (breaking at the loin, lowering head and neck); hind legs coming deeply under the body and locking in; front legs remaining mobile and walking forward; the horse staying straight (no sideways drift); smoothness and length of slide; and the horse completing the stop with willingness and without resistance. Faults include bouncing, veering sideways, raising the head, opening the mouth excessively, or stopping too short of the arena marker.

What does "credit" mean in reining scoring?+

In reining, "credit" is given for maneuvers performed with exceptional quality — smoothness, finesse, attitude, quickness, authority, and controlled speed. A horse that performs the required maneuvers with greater speed and difficulty, while maintaining correctness, earns positive maneuver scores above zero. Increased difficulty — faster spins, longer slides, more dramatic speed changes in circles — is rewarded when executed correctly. A horse that does everything correctly but without expression, speed, or difficulty scores a zero (average) on each maneuver.

How many judges score a reining class?+

Most NRHA approved shows use one or two judges. Major events like the NRHA Futurity and Derby use two judges. Each judge maintains an independent score sheet with maneuver scores and penalties for every horse. With two judges, the final score is determined by the average of the two judges' scores (or the sum, depending on show conditions). Each judge's score sheet is posted after the class for exhibitors to review.

What is a maneuver score?+

A maneuver score is the score assigned by the judge for each individual maneuver in the pattern. Every pattern has multiple maneuvers (typically 7-8), and each receives its own score from -1.5 to +1.5 in half-point increments. These maneuver scores are added together and then penalties are subtracted to arrive at the final score. The maneuver scores are posted on the judge's sheet after the class, allowing exhibitors to see exactly how each element of their run was evaluated.

What is a re-ride in reining?+

A re-ride is granted by the judge when an unavoidable circumstance disrupts a horse and rider's pattern — such as a loose horse entering the arena, arena equipment failure, or other interference completely outside the competitor's control. The rider may accept or decline the re-ride. If accepted, the original score is thrown out and the re-ride score stands. Re-rides are rare and granted solely at the judge's discretion.

How do I become an NRHA judge?+

Becoming an NRHA judge requires: submitting an application by October 15 (for January seminars) or December 15 (for April seminars); paying a $250 application fee; providing references from a current NRHA judge and a professional character reference; passing the Judges Applicant Seminar; and attending an NRHA Judges School. Applicants are reviewed by the NRHA Judges Committee. International seminars are held in conjunction with NRHA events worldwide. See nrha.com/judges for current application deadlines.

What is a show steward?+

An NRHA Show Steward is an official who serves as a liaison between exhibitors, show management, and judges at approved events. Stewards are responsible for: ensuring NRHA rules are followed; fielding complaints about unsportsmanlike conduct or horse abuse; observing warm-up areas for rule violations; and reporting any issues to the appropriate officials. Stewards undergo NRHA training and certification and play a critical role in maintaining the integrity and horse welfare standards at sanctioned events.

What is a scribe in reining?+

A scribe is a person who records the judge's maneuver scores and penalty marks on the official score sheet during each run. As the judge calls out scores quietly (typically using hand signals or a microphone in the judge's ear), the scribe records each mark in real time. After the run, the scribe tallies the scores and submits the sheet to the show office for official tabulation. Scribes are typically experienced exhibitors or officials who understand the scoring system thoroughly.

Can a judge's decision be appealed?+

NRHA rules state that all judges' decisions are final. There is no formal appeal process for maneuver scores or penalty assessments. However, exhibitors may file a protest with the show steward for procedural violations (such as a judge scoring a wrong pattern or a scoring error). Mathematical errors on the score sheet can be corrected through the show office. Exhibitors are encouraged to review posted judge's sheets after each class to understand how their run was scored.

What is a go-round in reining?+

A go-round is one complete round of competition in which all entered horses run once. Major events like the NRHA Futurity and Derby typically have multiple go-rounds (preliminary rounds) before the finals. Scores from go-rounds may be used to qualify horses for finals, or finals scores may stand alone — depending on the specific show conditions. The term "go" is also used informally to describe a single competitive run.

What is a Level 4 Open vs Level 1 Open?+

NRHA divides Open competition into levels based on a rider's lifetime earnings, protecting lower-earning riders from competing against top professionals. Level 4 Open is open to all professional riders regardless of earnings — this is the elite, premier class with the highest purses. Level 3, 2, and 1 Open have earnings caps that prevent riders above those thresholds from entering. Exact earning caps change periodically — check the current NRHA Eligibility Policy at nrha.com/eligibility for current limits.

03

MANEUVERS

14 Questions
What are all the maneuvers in reining?+

The core reining maneuvers are: Sliding stops — running to a halt with hind legs locked and sliding; Spins (turnarounds) — 360-degree rotations over a stationary hind foot; Rollbacks — 180-degree direction reversals over the hocks; Circles — large fast and small slow loping circles showing speed control; Flying lead changes — switching the leading leg at the lope without breaking gait; Rundowns — galloping the length of the arena to approach a stop; Back-ups — required in some patterns; Walk-ins or jog-ins — entering the arena; Hesitations — pauses at required pattern points.

How long should a sliding stop be?+

NRHA rules require the sliding stop to be performed at least 20 feet (6 meters) from the wall or fence. Elite competitors regularly achieve slides of 20 to 30+ feet. The length of the slide depends on the horse's speed, power, correct body position, the condition of sliding plates, and arena footing preparation. Longer slides with correct form earn more credit than short slides, even if technically correct.

How many spins are in a reining pattern?+

NRHA patterns typically require 4 spins in one direction followed by 4 spins in the opposite direction (though some patterns call for 4 and 4, or other combinations). Over or under-spinning by 1/8 turn results in a 0.5-point penalty; by 1/8 to 1/4 turn results in a 1-point penalty; by more than 1/4 turn results in a zero score for the pattern. Riders must count rotations precisely while simultaneously managing the horse's speed, pivot foot, and body position.

What is a pivot foot in a spin?+

The pivot foot (also called the inside hind foot) is the stationary rear leg around which the horse rotates during a spin. The horse's weight shifts to this inside hind foot, which should remain planted in place or very nearly so throughout the spin. The outside hind leg and both front legs provide propulsion. A horse that walks or hops with the pivot foot, or that moves it significantly during the spin, will lose credit. A clean, planted pivot foot that stays in one spot is a hallmark of a correctly trained reining horse.

What is the difference between large fast and small slow circles?+

NRHA patterns require both large fast circles and small slow circles, and the contrast between them is judged. Large fast circles are bigger in diameter and performed at a brisk, extended lope — the horse should cover ground and show speed. Small slow circles are tighter, more collected, and performed at a clearly slower speed. The judge rewards horses that show dramatic, obvious speed differentiation between the two, demonstrating the rider's control and the horse's responsiveness. A horse that shows little or no speed difference between large and small circles scores poorly.

What is a counter-arc in reining?+

Counter-arc is a training concept (not a specific competition maneuver) in which the horse's body is bent to the outside of the direction of travel — the opposite of the natural arc. It is used in training to develop suppleness, balance, and lateral flexibility. A horse that can travel counter-arced while maintaining correct lead and speed demonstrates advanced collection and responsiveness. Counter-arc work helps prepare horses for the precision required in competition circles and lead changes.

What is a simple lead change vs a flying lead change?+

A flying lead change happens while the horse is in motion at the lope — the horse switches its leading legs without breaking to a trot or walk. This is required in NRHA competition. A simple lead change breaks gait to a trot or walk between the two leads. Simple lead changes are penalized 1 point in NRHA competition. Young horses or entry-level programs (like NRHA Green classes) may allow simple changes as they develop. At the competitive level, flying changes are expected and must be simultaneous (front and hind legs switching at the same time).

What does "out of lead" mean and what is the penalty?+

A horse is "out of lead" when it is loping on the wrong lead for the direction of travel — for example, on the right lead while traveling in a left circle. Penalties are: out of lead for up to 1/4 circle = 0.5 point penalty; 1/4 to 1/2 circle = 1 point; 1/2 to 3/4 circle = 2 points; more than 3/4 circle or if the horse appears to have completed the circle on the wrong lead = zero score. Correct lead is fundamental and judges evaluate it closely throughout circles and lead changes.

What is a backup in reining?+

A backup is when the horse walks backward in a straight line, required in some NRHA patterns (particularly Pattern 7 and others). The horse should move backward in a straight line with willingness and responsiveness, maintaining correct head position and softness in the bridle. Judges evaluate straightness, willingness, and the number of steps required. A crooked or resistant backup loses credit.

What is a half-pass or two-track in reining training?+

Though not a competition maneuver, lateral movements like the half-pass (two-tracking) are valuable training tools for reining horses. Moving the horse sideways while traveling forward develops body control, suppleness, and responsiveness to leg pressure — all essential for precise circle shapes and correct spins. Many top reining trainers incorporate dressage-influenced lateral work into their conditioning programs.

What is a "big stop" in reining?+

A "big stop" is informal language for a sliding stop performed with maximum speed, commitment, and slide distance — one that generates crowd reaction. Big stops are the most crowd-pleasing moments in reining and typically earn the highest maneuver scores. A horse known for its stopping ability is considered one of the sport's most valuable competitors. Events like the 100X Super Slide feature special competitions specifically showcasing horses' stopping ability.

What is a Super Slide competition?+

A Super Slide is a head-to-head stopping competition in which horses compete purely on the quality and length of their sliding stop — no full pattern required. Competitors run down the arena and stop; judges (or sometimes crowd reaction) determine the winner. The 100X Brazos Slide in Waco features the $50,000 Clark Reining Horses and 21 Reining Horses Super Slide, one of the richest stopping competitions in the sport. Super Slides are fan favorites due to their simple, dramatic format.

What is a snaffle bit class in reining?+

Snaffle bit classes are for younger or less-finished horses that are still in early training stages. Horses in snaffle bit classes may be shown with two hands on the reins (unlike finished bridle horse classes that require one hand). These classes allow riders to use more direct rein communication while the horse develops its training. The NRHA has specific snaffle bit class conditions — check current show conditions for age and equipment requirements.

What is a hackamore class in reining?+

A hackamore is a bitless headstall that uses nose and chin pressure rather than a bit in the horse's mouth. Hackamore classes are a traditional part of western horsemanship and represent an intermediate stage in some training traditions between the snaffle and the finished bridle horse. Horses shown in hackamores may also be shown two-handed. NRHA has specific equipment rules for hackamore classes including restrictions on the type and weight of the hackamore used.

PATTERNS

15 Questions
How many NRHA approved patterns are there?+

NRHA has 20 approved patterns in their current handbook. Patterns 1 through 18 are used in standard Open and Non Pro competition. Pattern A and Pattern B are used exclusively for Youth and Para-Reining classes. Show management selects which pattern will be used for each class, and all horses in that class run the same pattern. AQHA and APHA may use their own patterns at breed-specific shows — always confirm which association's pattern is being used before showing.

How are patterns announced at shows?+

The pattern for each class is posted in the show office and/or on the show grounds well in advance — typically the morning of or the evening before the class. Competitors are responsible for knowing the pattern before entering the arena. Some shows also announce the pattern number in the show program. Riders may walk the arena on foot before competition to visualize circle sizes, stop locations, and spin positions. The pattern number is typically included in the class conditions at the time of entry.

Can you look at the pattern during your run?+

No — riders must have the pattern memorized before entering the arena. There is no reference material allowed during the run. Forgetting or performing maneuvers out of order is a pattern deviation that results in penalties or a zero score. Competitors typically memorize patterns by walking them on foot, mentally rehearsing, and watching other competitors run the same pattern before their own turn.

What are the arena markers used for in reining?+

NRHA competition arenas have markers on the fence at the center and 15 meters (49 feet) from each end wall. These markers guide riders while performing patterns — for example, indicating where to begin a rundown, where circles should be centered, and how far past the end wall a horse must run before stopping. Running short of the designated marker before a stop results in a 2-point penalty. Markers are typically cones, flags, or painted lines on the fence.

What happens if you run the wrong pattern?+

Running the wrong pattern is a major deviation that typically results in a zero score. All horses in a class run the same pattern — if a rider begins running a different pattern than the one designated for that class, they will receive a zero. This underscores the importance of confirming the correct pattern number before competing. Riders are solely responsible for knowing and performing the correct pattern.

What is a walk-in pattern vs a jog-in pattern?+

Some NRHA patterns begin with the horse walking to the center of the arena before starting, while others begin with the horse jogging (trotting) to the center. The pattern conditions specify which is required. Show management may also require all horses to jog to the center to assist with the show schedule — if so, this must be posted visibly at the show. The horse must walk or come to a complete stop before beginning the pattern. Judging begins as soon as the horse enters the arena.

What is a hesitation point in a pattern?+

Hesitation points are specific moments in the pattern where the horse must pause briefly before continuing — most commonly after completing a set of spins, after a stop at the end of a rundown, or at the beginning and end of the overall pattern. The hesitation allows the judge to see the maneuver clearly completed before the horse moves to the next element. Failing to hesitate where the pattern requires it may result in a penalty.

How large is a reining arena?+

NRHA does not specify a single required arena size, but recommends a minimum of 100 x 200 feet for competition. Larger arenas allow more room for circles and longer rundowns. The major events (Futurity, Derby, Run For A Million) use arenas significantly larger than the minimum. The footing must be properly prepared — typically a sandy mix that is groomed, watered, and leveled — to allow safe sliding stops without excessive compaction or deep footing that could injure horses.

What is the "center of the arena" in a reining pattern?+

The center of the arena is the designated starting and transition point for most reining patterns. Horses walk or jog to the center before beginning, lead changes are made at the center when changing circle direction, and many patterns require stops or hesitations at the center. The center marker is typically a cone or painted mark at the midpoint of the arena length and width. Riders must have a strong visual sense of where the center is to execute patterns correctly.

Are patterns different at AQHA shows vs NRHA shows?+

Yes — AQHA (American Quarter Horse Association) and APHA (American Paint Horse Association) may use their own approved patterns at breed shows, which can differ from NRHA-approved patterns. NRHA approved shows must use one of the 20 NRHA-approved patterns. If a show is dually sanctioned (both NRHA and AQHA, for example), the NRHA pattern is typically used. Always confirm which rulebook governs the class you are entering before showing.

What is Pattern 1 in NRHA competition?+

Pattern 1 is one of the most commonly used NRHA patterns at approved shows. It begins with the horse running at speed to the far end of the arena, executing a sliding stop, rolling back, and then performing circles, spins, and additional stops following the prescribed sequence. Each of the 20 NRHA patterns sequences the core maneuvers differently to test the horse and rider in varied orders and configurations. Pattern 1 is straightforward enough for newer competitors to learn but demands the same quality execution as any NRHA pattern.

What is the most difficult NRHA pattern?+

Difficulty is subjective and depends on the horse and rider's strengths, but patterns requiring multiple direction changes, alternating circles and rundowns, and complex spin sequences are generally considered more demanding. Patterns used at Futurity and Derby finals challenge the best horses and riders in the world. Those requiring the most transitions between maneuvers and the most precise spatial awareness tend to be the most challenging for competitors at all levels.

Can I practice reining patterns at home?+

Yes, but with important caveats. Practicing full patterns repeatedly can cause horses to anticipate maneuvers, leading to resistance in competition. Many trainers recommend schooling individual maneuvers separately rather than running complete patterns daily. When patterns are practiced, trainers often vary the pattern, change directions, or interrupt at different points to prevent anticipation. Full pattern runs are typically reserved for show conditions rather than daily schooling.

What are the arena dimensions for reining competition?+

NRHA recommends a minimum arena size of approximately 100 x 200 feet (30 x 60 meters) for competition, with markers at the center and 15 meters from each end wall. Major events like the Futurity and Derby use significantly larger arenas at the OKC Fairgrounds, allowing for longer rundowns and more dramatic stops. The footing must be carefully prepared — typically a sand-based mix groomed and watered throughout the competition to allow safe, consistent sliding stops.

What is a Freestyle reining pattern?+

In Freestyle reining, riders design their own pattern performed to music. The pattern must incorporate required maneuvers (stops, spins, rollbacks, circles, lead changes) but the sequence and choreography are the rider's choice. Freestyle scoring combines technical merit with artistic impression — music, choreography, and showmanship all factor in. Riders often use costumes and props. The best Freestyle runs are among the most entertaining spectacles in all of equestrian sport.

COMPETITION & MAJOR EVENTS

18 Questions
What is the NRHA Futurity?+

The NRHA Futurity is the most prestigious event in reining — called "the pinnacle event of the industry." Held annually in late November/early December at the OKC Fairgrounds in Oklahoma City, it is restricted to 3-year-old horses only. The 2025 Futurity set an all-time purse record of $3.9 million combined (Futurity + Futurity Challenge), surpassing the previous record by over $722,000. In 2026, the Futurity celebrates its 60th anniversary. Dates: November 29 – December 7, 2026.

What is the NRHA Derby?+

The 6666 NRHA Derby presented by Markel is the sport's second-largest event and the premier summer competition. Held annually in June at the OKC Fairgrounds, it is open to 4, 5, 6, and 7-year-old horses. The 2026 Derby runs June 8–21 at the State Fair Arena in Oklahoma City and also includes the rescheduled 2025 Adequan North American Affiliate Championships (NAAC) due to the EHV postponement from 2025. The Derby pays $3 million or more in combined purses.

What is the Run For A Million?+

The Run For A Million (TRFAM) is the richest event in reining history, held annually at the South Point Arena and Equestrian Center in Las Vegas. What is a Non Pro class in reining?+

A Non Pro (Non Professional) class is for amateur riders who do not earn money from training, showing, or instructing horses. Non Pros must not accept any compensation related to horses during the eligibility period. Non Pro classes allow amateur competitors to compete against others of similar status rather than against full-time professionals. Like Open classes, Non Pro is divided into levels (Level 1–4) based on lifetime earnings. The NRHA Non Pro Futurity is one of the most competitive amateur horse sport divisions in the world.

What is a Prime Time class?+

NRHA MEMBERSHIP & RULES

12 Questions
Do I need to be an NRHA member to show?+

Yes — to compete in NRHA approved shows, riders and horses must be current NRHA members. Horse owners, riders, and trainers each need appropriate memberships. NRHA membership types include General Member, Non Pro Member, Associate Member, and Youth Member. Membership provides access to approved show competition, year-end awards programs, standings, and the online ReinerSuite platform. Join at nrha.com/join.

What equipment is legal in NRHA competition?+

Legal bits include curb bits with shanks (for finished bridle horses shown one-handed) and snaffle bits (for two-handed snaffle classes). Hackamores are permitted in designated hackamore classes. Western saddles, pad, and standard western show attire are required. Boots, chaps, and western hats are customary. Illegal equipment includes: mechanical hackamores in bridle horse classes; bits with shanks exceeding legal measurements; certain tie-down or restrictive devices. The NRHA Handbook contains detailed equipment specifications including bit measurement diagrams.

What are the NRHA medication rules?+

NRHA enforces strict medication rules to protect horse welfare. Prohibited substances include performance-enhancing drugs, pain-masking medications, and anything that could compromise a horse's ability to feel pain during competition. Horses may be drug tested at any NRHA approved show. Violations result in disqualification, fines, and suspension. NRHA's welfare and medication policies are detailed in the official handbook. See nrha.com/medications for the current approved and prohibited substance list.

What is the NRHA Handbook?+

The NRHA Handbook is the official rulebook governing all aspects of NRHA-sanctioned competition. It covers: judging rules and scoring; approved patterns; equipment specifications; eligibility requirements; show conditions; medication and welfare policies; nomination and entry procedures; and year-end award programs. The handbook is updated periodically and is available free at nrha.com/handbook. Any competitor, trainer, judge, or official should be thoroughly familiar with the current handbook.

What is NRHA eligibility and how does it affect what I can show?+

NRHA eligibility determines which competitive levels a rider may enter based on lifetime earnings in NRHA competition. Riders who exceed the earning threshold for a level (1, 2, 3, or 4) must move up to the next level. Non Pro eligibility also takes into account whether riders receive any compensation related to horses. Eligibility is checked and updated regularly by NRHA. It is each competitor's responsibility to compete in the correct level — competing in a level below your eligibility is a serious violation. Check your current eligibility at nrha.com/eligibility.

What is ReinerSuite?+

ReinerSuite is the NRHA's online member portal available at reinersuite.nrha.com. Members use it to manage memberships and renewals, view lifetime earnings and eligibility status, access show results and standings, register horses, and manage nominations. Show managers use ReinerSuite to submit results to NRHA. It is the central database for all NRHA competitive records and the authoritative source for eligibility determinations.

How does the NRHA year-end awards program work?+

NRHA tracks points earned at approved shows throughout the competition year. Points are accumulated by class and level, and year-end high-point winners are recognized with awards, prizes, and recognition at major events. The year-end standings are maintained through ReinerSuite. To be eligible for year-end awards, both the horse and rider must be current NRHA members. Specific point values and award categories are detailed in the current NRHA Handbook.

What is the NRHA Apprentice program?+

The NRHA Apprentice program is a structured pathway for aspiring professional trainers to develop their skills under the mentorship of established NRHA professionals. Apprentices work toward earning their professional trainer status while gaining hands-on experience, competing, and building their reputation in the industry. The program provides a formal framework for entry into the professional ranks of reining. Details at nrha.com/apprentice.

What is the NRHA Hall of Fame?+

The NRHA Hall of Fame honors the individuals and horses who have made the greatest contributions to the sport of reining throughout its history. Inductees include legendary trainers, riders, horses, owners, and contributors to the sport's growth. The Hall of Fame is located in Oklahoma City and induction is one of the sport's highest honors. NRHA announces new inductees each year and holds an induction ceremony during the Futurity week. View the Hall of Fame at nrha.com/hall-fame.

What is the Dale Wilkinson Lifetime Achievement Award?+

The Dale Wilkinson Lifetime Achievement Award is the NRHA's highest honor for individuals who have made extraordinary lifetime contributions to the sport of reining, named after one of the sport's most influential early figures. It is awarded infrequently and only to individuals whose contributions to reining span decades and have fundamentally shaped the sport's growth, governance, or culture. It is announced alongside Hall of Fame inductees each year.

What are the NRHA past champions resources?+

NRHA maintains complete historical records of past champions at all major events including the Futurity, Derby, and other sanctioned competitions going back decades. The sport also has dedicated resources like WorldsGreatestHorseman.com which chronicles NRCHA champions, and individual event websites that maintain their own historical results.

Yes — NRHA events are open to competitors from around the world. The organization sanctions events in Europe, Australia, Canada, Mexico, South America, and beyond. TRAINING & EQUIPMENT

14 Questions
How long does it take to train a reining horse?+

Training a reining horse from scratch typically takes 2 to 3 years of consistent, progressive work before the horse is competitive at the higher levels. Young horses typically start under saddle at 18–24 months, learn basic foundation work through their 2-year-old year, and begin competing as 3-year-olds in Futurity classes. Elite horses continue developing through their 4, 5, 6, and 7-year-old years as they compete in the Derby and aged events. A truly finished bridle horse — performing at the highest level with lightness and expression — may take 4 to 5+ years of training.

What is a professional reining trainer and how do I find one?+

A professional reining trainer is someone who trains, conditions, and shows reining horses for clients as their primary profession. Top trainers have NRHA professional status, significant competitive records, and established reputations. To find an NRHA certified professional trainer, visit nrha.com/professional-trainers for the official directory. When selecting a trainer, consider their competitive record, training philosophy, facility, references, and whether they are a good fit for your goals and budget.

What is a turnaround / spin training progression?+

Spins are typically developed in stages: first teaching the horse to move laterally off leg pressure; then introducing the concept of pivoting on the inside hind foot with forward movement; then developing the crossing motion of the front feet; and finally building speed, consistency, and precision. Trainers use various techniques including positioning against a fence, working in a small circle, and gradual speed development. A horse rushing or losing the pivot foot is a common training challenge that requires returning to slower, more deliberate work.

How do you teach a sliding stop?+

Teaching the stop begins long before a horse is asked to slide. Foundation work includes: teaching the horse to respond to a "whoa" cue at the walk and trot; building body position (breaking at the loin, lowering the hindquarters) at slow speeds; developing the horse's confidence and willingness to run forward before stopping; gradually increasing speed as the horse learns the correct body position; and fitting the horse with sliding plates to allow the hind feet to glide. Rushing the stop before the horse understands body position creates resistance and training problems that are difficult to correct.

What is a bridle horse?+

A "bridle horse" is a finished, highly trained reining horse that performs all maneuvers in a curb bit with one hand on the reins. Showing in a bridle horse class means the horse has advanced beyond the snaffle and hackamore stages and performs with the finesse and lightness expected of a fully trained western horse. The term has roots in the California vaquero tradition where horses progressed through the snaffle, hackamore, and finally the spade bit or curb bit — each stage representing greater refinement and subtlety of communication.

What kind of saddle is used in reining?+

Reining saddles are a specialized type of western saddle designed for the demands of the sport. They feature: a deep seat for security during stops and spins; a higher cantle that supports the rider's lower back in the stop; minimal bulk under the fender to allow close leg contact; a free-swinging fender that allows the leg to move freely; and typically a flatter seat compared to working ranch saddles. Major manufacturers include Cactus Saddlery, Big Horn, Circle Y, and Dale Chavez. Many elite competitors have custom-built saddles fitted to both horse and rider.

What kind of bit is used in reining?+

Reining uses curb bits with shanks for finished bridle horses (shown one-handed) and snaffle bits for horses in earlier training stages (shown two-handed). The NRHA Handbook specifies legal shank lengths and port heights. Common curb bit styles include the Argentine or correction bit and various custom designs. Many trainers use a progression: direct-rein snaffle → indirect-rein hackamore → finished curb bit. Illegal bits or bits outside measurement specifications result in disqualification.

What are sliding plates and who puts them on?+

Sliding plates are specialized smooth-soled horseshoes applied only to a reining horse's hind feet. They are wider, longer, and thicker than standard shoes with no toe clips or side clips that would catch in the ground. Applied by a qualified farrier (typically one experienced with reining horses), sliding plates must be properly fitted and the horse's hoof must be in correct balance to allow maximum slide without injury. Most competition horses are re-shod every 6–8 weeks. The farrier relationship is one of the most important partnerships in a reining program.

What footing is used in reining arenas?+

Reining arena footing is typically a carefully prepared mixture of sand, dirt, and sometimes synthetic materials designed to allow long, smooth sliding stops without injuring horses. The footing must be: deep enough to slide (typically 3–4 inches); firm enough to provide traction for spins and circles; free of hard spots or rocks; uniformly groomed and leveled; and properly dampened to control dust and maintain consistency. Major events employ professional footing management teams. Poor footing is a significant factor in horse injuries and is closely monitored by NRHA officials.

What is the typical cost to show a reining horse?+

Costs vary widely by level. Entry-level affiliate shows might cost $50–150 per class. Regional NRHA shows run $200–500+ per class including entry fees, judges fees, office fees, and stall costs. Major events like the NRHA Derby can run $1,500–2,500+ for a single Open entry. Add boarding, transportation, farrier, veterinary, and training fees on top of entry costs. Competing at the Futurity or Derby level with a professional trainer managing the horse can cost $30,000–$80,000+ per year including all expenses. Entry Level and affiliate shows offer much more affordable paths to get started.

What conditioning does a reining horse need?+

Reining horses require a balanced conditioning program that builds cardiovascular fitness, muscular strength (particularly in the hindquarters, back, and core), suppleness, and mental sharpness. Training typically includes: daily arena work (pattern practice, maneuver development); wet work or swimming for low-impact conditioning; longing or ground work; hacking outside the arena for mental relaxation; and periodic veterinary care including joint maintenance, dental care, and chiropractic or massage therapy. Overtraining is a common cause of injury — top trainers carefully balance work and rest.

What are common reining horse injuries?+

The demands of stopping and spinning place significant stress on reining horses' hind limbs and joints. Common injuries include: stifle problems (EPM, OCD, or soft tissue injury); hock issues (bone spavin or soft tissue strain); suspensory ligament strains; back soreness; and front end issues from the repetitive impact of spins. Proper conditioning, appropriate footing, regular veterinary assessment, and thoughtful training management significantly reduce injury risk. Many top programs use regular joint maintenance injections, ultrasound monitoring, and preventive veterinary care.

What is "Buy A Pro" in NRHA?+

Buy A Pro is an NRHA program that connects Non Pro exhibitors with professional trainers for educational experiences. Non Pros can purchase rides or training time with top NRHA professionals, gaining hands-on guidance from the sport's elite competitors. It is a popular educational tool that helps Non Pros improve their own riding while supporting professional trainers. Information about the program is available at nrha.com/buy-a-pro.

What attire do reining competitors wear?+

NRHA requires western attire for all competitors. Standard show attire includes: a western hat or approved helmet; western show shirt (often with elaborate embroidery or rhinestones); chaps or chinks; western boots; and gloves (optional but common). Women often wear sparkle and rhinestone-accented shirts. Men typically wear plainer show shirts. NRHA rules specify minimum attire requirements — no specific style is mandated beyond western dress. Some competitors choose a clean, classic western look while others go for more elaborate show-ring presentation.

HORSES & BREEDS

12 Questions
What breed is best for reining?+

The American Quarter Horse dominates reining competition due to its natural athleticism, powerful hindquarters, low center of gravity, quick reflexes, and trainable temperament. Quarter Horses are bred specifically for the explosive speed, lateral agility, and stoppability required in reining. American Paint Horses and Appaloosas also compete regularly in open NRHA events. At AQHA or APHA breed shows, only registered horses of those breeds may compete. In open NRHA competition, any breed may enter — but Quarter Horse bloodlines dominate at the elite level.

What conformation makes a good reining horse?+

Ideal reining horse conformation includes: a long, sloping hip with powerful muscling (drives the sliding stop); a short, strong back; well-angulated hocks and stifles (allows deep engagement under the body); a relatively low hock set; a long hip from point of hip to point of buttock; correct, straight legs with good bone; a short, flexible back; an athletic, "boxy" build overall; and a tractable, willing temperament. Horses with overly straight hocks ("post-legged") or insufficient hip angle struggle to generate the deep engagement needed for quality stops.

How much does a reining horse cost?+

Prices vary enormously. A well-bred, untrained weanling from top bloodlines might cost $5,000–$25,000. A started 2-year-old heading to the Futurity might be $25,000–$100,000. A proven competition horse with NRHA earnings can range from $50,000 to $500,000+. Elite horses that have won at the Futurity or Derby level — or produced winners — may sell for $250,000 to over $1 million. The Markel Futurity Sale held in conjunction with the NRHA Futurity is one of the premier reining horse sales in the world.

What age can a horse start reining training?+

Most reining horses begin light groundwork and handling as weanlings and yearlings, then start under saddle at 18–24 months. Serious training typically begins in earnest as 2-year-olds, with competition beginning as 3-year-olds at Futurity-level events. Some trainers prefer to wait until horses are more physically mature (2.5–3 years) before beginning heavy training demands. The physical stress of sliding stops and spins on young, still-developing horses must be managed carefully to prevent early injury and career-ending soundness issues.

What is a "futurity horse"?+

A futurity horse is a 3-year-old horse being prepared specifically to compete at the NRHA Futurity or other 3-year-old aged events. The futurity horse program is one of the most intense in equine sports — young horses are trained rapidly to perform at a high level within a narrow window of eligibility. Trainers specializing in futurity horses have a specific skill set for developing young horses quickly while managing their physical and mental wellbeing. Many of the sport's most famous horses made their name as futurity competitors.

What is a derby horse?+

A derby horse is typically a 4 to 7-year-old competing in the NRHA Derby or similar aged events. After the Futurity (3-year-olds), horses advance to the Derby division where they continue developing and refining their skills over multiple years of competition. Many horses that were good futurity prospects become exceptional derby horses as they mature physically and mentally. The Derby allows horses to develop at their own pace over four years of eligibility, rewarding those that continue to improve with age.

What is a stallion in the reining industry?+

Stallions play a massive commercial role in reining — top sires can earn millions of dollars in breeding fees based on their competitive record and the success of their offspring. Stallion owners invest heavily in competition to demonstrate their horses' genetics, then market breeding rights to mare owners. Major programs like the NRHA Nomination Program, 100X Invitational (exclusive to nominated stallion offspring), and Sire and Dam classes at major events are built around the stallion breeding economy. A single breeding fee to a top stallion can range from $2,500 to $25,000+.

What is a Novice Horse class?+

A Novice Horse class is for horses with limited NRHA lifetime earnings — typically horses that have not won more than $10,000 in NRHA lifetime earnings as of a specific date. These classes level the playing field, preventing highly experienced horses from competing against true newcomers. The NRHA Derby, for example, offers Novice Horse Open and Novice Horse Non Pro divisions that run concurrently with the main Derby go-rounds. Cross-entry into the main Derby is allowed but not required.

What is a mare vs gelding vs stallion in reining competition?+

All three sexes compete in open NRHA classes with no separation by sex. Geldings (castrated males) are particularly common in reining due to their typically consistent, even temperament — they are not affected by hormonal cycles. Mares can be excellent competitors but some trainers find managing mares' heat cycles adds complexity. Stallions are the least common competitors in open classes due to management challenges and the value of their time for breeding, though successful competing stallions command premium breeding fees based on their record.

How do I evaluate a reining horse before buying?+

Before purchasing a reining horse: have an independent veterinarian perform a full pre-purchase exam including flexion tests and digital radiographs of key joints; verify NRHA registration and earnings records through ReinerSuite; watch the horse perform its maneuvers at various speeds; ride the horse yourself if appropriate; review the horse's show record and veterinary history; understand any current joint maintenance protocols; and work with an experienced trainer or industry professional who can evaluate the horse objectively. The reining horse market moves fast — a trusted advisor is invaluable.

What is the NRHA Stallion Directory?+

The NRHA Stallion Directory at nrha.com/stallion-directory is the official listing of NRHA-registered breeding stallions. Mare owners use it to research pedigrees, earnings records, and breeding fees of available stallions. Many commercial stallion programs also advertise through Reiner Magazine, at major events, and through breed association publications. The stallion market is a significant economic driver in the reining industry.

What is the Reining Horse Foundation?+

The Reining Horse Foundation (RHF) is the nonprofit charitable arm of NRHA that supports youth programs, educational initiatives, horse welfare, and crisis assistance for members of the reining community. The RHF Crisis Fund provides financial assistance to reining community members facing hardship — including, most recently, assistance following severe winter storms in 2026. The Foundation is funded through donations, fundraising events, and a percentage of NRHA show proceeds. Learn more at reiningfoundation.com.

BLOODLINES & STALLIONS

15 Questions
What bloodlines dominate reining?+

Modern reining bloodlines trace back to a handful of foundation Quarter Horse sires. The dominant bloodlines in current competition include lines from Topsail Whiz, Gunner (Mr Gun Smoke), Spooks Gona Sting, Smart Chic Olena, Colonels Smoky, Wimpys Little Step, Dun It With Class, and more recently Magnum Chic Dream and Be Bop Bol. These stallions produced horses that excel at the combination of athletic stopping ability, speed control, and trainability that defines elite reining. The 100X Invitational was specifically designed to showcase emerging top sires.

Who is Gunner (Mr Gun Smoke)?+

Gunner (Mr Gun Smoke) is one of the most influential reining stallions of all time. He was an NRHA Futurity and Derby champion ridden by Tim McQuay and later produced offspring that dominated competition for over a decade. Gunner's offspring are known for their natural stopping ability, athletic build, and willingness. His impact on modern reining bloodlines is immeasurable — he is considered one of the sport's greatest foundation sires of the modern era.

Who is Colonels Shining Gun?+

Colonels Shining Gun is a record-setting reining sire who crossed the $4 million milestone in sire earnings — one of the highest totals in NRHA history. His offspring have won at the Futurity, Derby, and Run For A Million levels. He represents the next generation of elite reining sires whose influence is being seen across the sport at every level of competition. His breeding program is one of the most sought-after in the industry.

What is Smart Chic Olena?+

Smart Chic Olena is a legendary reining and cow horse stallion whose breeding appears throughout modern reining pedigrees. He was a champion competitor and then one of the sport's most prolific and influential sires. His combination of cow sense, athletic ability, and trainability — inherited from his dam Smart Little Lena (one of the all-time great cutting horse sires) — made him extraordinarily valuable as a breeding stallion. Many current top stallions trace back to Smart Chic Olena within a generation or two.

What is the Topsail bloodline?+

The Topsail line traces to Topsail Whiz, one of the most successful and influential reining stallions of the 1990s and 2000s. Topsail Whiz was a multiple Futurity and Derby champion and produced countless successful offspring. The Topsail name became synonymous with athletic, stoppable horses with excellent minds. His son Whizards Baby Doll was also influential, and the line continues to be well-represented in elite competition through multiple generations.

What does NRHA sire earnings mean?+

NRHA sire earnings represent the total NRHA prize money won by all of a stallion's offspring combined throughout his stud career. It is one of the primary measures of a stallion's value as a breeding sire. Stallions that consistently produce winning offspring command premium breeding fees and generate enormous commercial value for their owners. A stallion crossing the $1 million, $2 million, or $4 million sire earnings mark is a major industry milestone widely celebrated and marketed.

What is the 100X Invitational and why is it important for stallion owners?+

The 100X Invitational is an exclusive 3-year-old class at the 100X Reining Classic that was specifically designed to promote elite junior stallions and their offspring. With a $750,000 purse, the Invitational provides a high-profile, high-money showcase for the next generation of breeding stallions. For stallion owners, having an Invitational winner dramatically increases breeding demand and fee levels. The class has become one of the most commercially significant events in reining in just its first few years.

What is a Spooks Gona Sting offspring?+

Spooks Gona Sting is a prominent reining stallion known for producing horses with exceptional natural stopping ability, correct conformation, and competitive spirit. His offspring have been consistently competitive at the Futurity and Derby levels and his influence continues to grow. Gabriel Borges, who surpassed $1 million in NRHA earnings following the 2025 Futurity, rode Spooks Gotta Ice — a son of Spooks Gona Sting — to win the 2026 Qualifier, demonstrating the sire's continued elite-level production.

How do I research a reining horse's pedigree?+

Quarter Horse and Paint Horse pedigrees can be researched through AQHA's online registry and AllBreedPedigree.com. NRHA earnings records are available through ReinerSuite. Equineline provides detailed racing and performance pedigree analysis. For breeding research, major reining farms and stallion stations publish pedigrees on their websites. When evaluating a reining prospect, cross-reference the horse's sire's NRHA sire earnings, dam's record, and the overall pattern of the pedigree going back 3–5 generations.

What role does the dam play in a reining horse's genetics?+

While stallions often get the commercial spotlight in reining, the dam (mother) contributes equally to a horse's genetics and significantly influences temperament, trainability, and athletic ability. Proven producer mares — those whose offspring have won significant NRHA earnings — are highly valuable. Some mare lines are so consistently productive that they command premium purchase prices and are maintained as broodmares long after their competitive careers end. When evaluating a young horse's potential, the dam's record and her dam's record are as important as the sire's.

What is Wimpys Little Step known for in reining?+

Wimpys Little Step is one of the most decorated reining stallions in history — an NRHA Futurity and Derby champion who also became a highly influential sire. Known for his incredible natural stopping ability, athletic build, and trainable disposition, Wimpys Little Step's offspring have competed at the highest levels of the sport for years. His bloodline is sought after by breeders looking for horses with natural "stop" — the raw athletic talent to slide without extensive training to achieve it.

What is Magnum Chic Dream known for?+

Magnum Chic Dream is a modern elite reining stallion whose offspring have become some of the most competitive horses at the Futurity and Derby in recent years. Known for producing horses with exceptional athleticism, natural collection, and competitive fire, Magnum Chic Dream represents the current generation of premier reining sires. His breeding program has grown rapidly based on the consistent success of his offspring at the highest levels of NRHA competition.

What role does the NRHA Markel Futurity Sale play?+

The NRHA Markel Futurity Sale is one of the premier reining horse sales in the world, held annually during Futurity week in Oklahoma City. Top 3-year-old horses — many fresh off competitive futurity runs — are sold to buyers from around the world. The sale provides a critical market for breeders and trainers to recoup their investment and for buyers to acquire proven or promising competition horses. Sale averages and top prices are closely watched as indicators of the overall health and momentum of the reining market.

What is an NRHA Million Dollar Owner?+

An NRHA Million Dollar Owner is a horse owner whose horses have collectively earned over $1 million in NRHA prize money. Like the Million Dollar Rider milestone for competitors, Million Dollar Owner status is one of the sport's most prestigious recognitions for those who invest in reining horses. Owners who achieve this milestone are recognized by NRHA and celebrated within the industry. Recent honorees include Rick Christen of Story Book Stables, recognized as NRHA's newest Million Dollar Owner in early 2026.

How do stallion nomination programs work in reining?+

Stallion nomination programs (like the NRHA Nomination Program and 100X Invitational) work by having stallion owners voluntarily enroll their stallions and pay annual fees. Mare owners who breed to enrolled stallions then nominate their foals and maintain payments at various stages (typically as weanlings, yearlings, and 2-year-olds). Nominated horses become eligible to compete in the associated futurity/invitational classes and share in the enhanced prize money generated by the nomination fees. Missing payment deadlines disqualifies the horse from the program, so careful record-keeping is essential.

YOUTH & AMATEUR

16 Questions

The NRHA Youth program (administered through NRHYA — the National Reining Horse Youth Association at nrhya.com) provides competitive opportunities, scholarships, and leadership development for reining competitors under 18 years of age. Youth classes are offered at all major NRHA events including the Futurity and Derby. The program includes Short Stirrup for very young riders, Youth 13 and Under, and Youth 14–18 divisions. Youth competitors may use simplified patterns and equipment concessions in some classes.

What age can a child start competing in reining?+

Children can begin competing in reining as young as 5–6 years old in Short Stirrup classes at affiliate shows, which use simplified patterns and small, calm horses. Youth 13 and Under classes accommodate riders up to 13, and Youth 14–18 covers the teenage years through high school. Many professional reining trainers run junior programs specifically designed for young riders. Some of the sport's top Non Pro competitors today started in youth classes as children and developed their skills over a lifetime of competition.

What is the Short Stirrup class?+

Short Stirrup is an NRHA youth division for the sport's youngest competitors — typically riders 10 years old and under, though age cutoffs vary by show. Short Stirrup classes use simplified, abbreviated patterns appropriate for young riders and typically smaller, quieter horses. The division is designed to introduce children to organized reining competition in a safe, age-appropriate environment. Pattern A and Pattern B are the specific NRHA patterns used for youth and para-reining classes.

What is Para-Reining?+

Para-Reining is an NRHA division for riders with physical disabilities. It uses Pattern B — a simplified reining pattern — and accommodates various adaptive needs. Para-Reining competitors may use adaptive equipment and have modified rules based on their specific disability classification. The NRHA is committed to making the sport accessible to riders of all abilities. Para-Reining classes are offered at select NRHA events and are a growing part of the sport's inclusive programming.

What is a Rookie class in reining?+

Rookie classes are for riders who are new to NRHA competition — typically those who have not yet accumulated significant NRHA earnings. Rookie 1 is for the most inexperienced competitors; Rookie 2 for those with slightly more experience. Rookie classes are featured at major events including the Run For A Million, where Rookie 1 and Rookie 2 competitors have their own challenges with a qualification path to Las Vegas. The Rookie program is one of NRHA's most successful initiatives for growing new participation in the sport.

How do I get started in reining as a complete beginner?+

Start with these steps: 1) Find an NRHA affiliate near you at nrha.com/affiliate-directory — many offer beginner clinics; 2) Take lessons from an NRHA professional trainer to learn the basics of seat, position, and communication; 3) Watch reining competitions in person or online to understand the patterns and maneuvers; 4) Start with Entry Level Ride and Slide shows before moving to NRHA approved competition; 5) Join NRHA at nrha.com/join; 6) Consider the Non Pro or Rookie classes for your first competition experience. Reining is genuinely welcoming to new participants at the affiliate level.

What NRHA scholarships are available for youth?+

The Reining Horse Foundation offers scholarships to NRHA Youth members pursuing higher education. Scholarship applications are typically accepted annually and are awarded based on academic achievement, community involvement, and involvement in the reining industry. The amount and number of scholarships awarded varies by year. Youth members should check reiningfoundation.com and nrhya.com for current scholarship opportunities and application deadlines.

What is the World's Greatest Youth Horseman competition?+

The World's Greatest Youth Horseman (presented by Western Bloodstock) is a prestigious multi-discipline youth competition in which the top 10 qualifiers are invited to compete at the Run For A Million in Las Vegas. Youth compete across multiple western performance disciplines to demonstrate all-around horsemanship skills. The event is one of the most coveted youth titles in western performance horses and provides a path to the Las Vegas spotlight for the sport's most talented young competitors.

Can a Non Pro rider also use a professional trainer?+

Yes — Non Pro eligibility refers to the competitor's status, not whether they employ a trainer. Non Pro riders regularly work with professional trainers to develop their horses and their own skills. What disqualifies Non Pro status is receiving personal financial compensation for training, showing, or instructing horses. A Non Pro may pay a trainer to train their horse and even to coach them, as long as the Non Pro themselves does not accept money for horse-related work. Consult the current NRHA Handbook for complete Non Pro eligibility definitions.

What is the NRHA Amateur class?+

Within some NRHA events (particularly the Derby), there is an Amateur division specifically for Non Pro riders competing on the same horse they entered in the Non Pro Derby. The Amateur class runs concurrently with the go-rounds with placings determined within the first go. It is an additional competitive opportunity for Non Pro competitors without requiring separate entry fees or additional runs. The same horse and rider must be entered in both the Non Pro Derby and the Amateur to be eligible.

What is the NRHA Non Pro Futurity?+

The NRHA Non Pro Futurity runs concurrently with the Open Futurity each December in Oklahoma City, featuring amateur riders competing on 3-year-old horses. The 2025 Non Pro Futurity wrapped with record-breaking competition and paid out over $1.1 million to Non Pro competitors — a testament to the depth and quality of amateur participation in the sport. The Non Pro Futurity is one of the most competitive amateur horse sport events in the world, with thousands of entries from across the globe.

How does reining compare to other western disciplines for youth?+

Reining offers youth several advantages: clear, objective scoring (70-point base system is easy to understand); patterns that can be learned and practiced at home; a welcoming community with strong youth programming; progression pathways from Entry Level to affiliate to NRHA competition; and significant prize money at the elite level for those who develop to top competitive level. Compared to barrel racing (more dependent on horse speed genetics) or cutting (requires expensive trained cattle horses), reining offers a structured, skills-based pathway that rewards dedicated practice and good horsemanship.

What is the NRHYA?+

The National Reining Horse Youth Association (NRHYA) is the youth division of NRHA, dedicated to developing the next generation of reining competitors and leaders. The NRHYA manages youth competition programs, year-end awards for youth competitors, scholarship programs, and leadership development initiatives. Youth members can participate in NRHYA programs through their NRHA membership. The organization's website at nrhya.com provides resources, competition calendars, and program information specifically for youth participants and their families.

What is the Non Pro card in NRHA?+

An NRHA Non Pro card is the official designation that allows a rider to compete in Non Pro classes. To hold Non Pro status, a rider must not receive any compensation related to horses — no training fees, no instruction fees, no showing other people's horses for pay. Riders who receive any horse-related income must compete as Open professionals. Once a rider gives up their Non Pro card to turn professional, reinstating Non Pro status requires meeting specific criteria and waiting periods outlined in the NRHA Handbook. Managing Non Pro eligibility carefully is critical for amateur competitors who value their Non Pro status.

What is a Select class in reining?+

Select classes in NRHA competition are divisions for competitors 50 years of age or older, similar to senior divisions in other sports. They allow experienced competitors to show against peers of similar age rather than competing against younger riders. Select Non Pro classes are offered at many NRHA-approved shows and at major events. They recognize that the sport has a dedicated community of lifelong participants who continue competing well into their 50s, 60s, and beyond — a testament to both reining's accessibility and its community.

How does reining score compare to other judged equestrian disciplines?+

Reining's 70-point base system is unique and relatively easy for newcomers to understand compared to some other judged disciplines. In dressage, scores are percentages of a maximum. In hunters, scoring is subjective and based on overall impression. In reining, the base of 70 with clear maneuver scores and penalty points makes it transparent — competitors can review their judge's sheet after a run and understand exactly where they lost or gained points. This transparency, combined with the dramatic visual appeal of stops and spins, makes reining one of the most spectator-friendly judged equestrian disciplines.
